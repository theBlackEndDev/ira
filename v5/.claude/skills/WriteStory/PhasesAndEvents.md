# Phases and Events — Unified Structural Map

The positional reference for story structure. This document maps WHERE every mandatory event falls in a story, organized by three acts and their internal phases. It synthesizes Syd Field, Blake Snyder (Save the Cat), Christopher Vogler (Hero's Journey), Steven Pressfield, Robert McKee, and Dan Harmon into a single unified timeline.

For the PHILOSOPHY behind these positions (why each framework works), see `StoryStructures.md`. For Pressfield's storytelling philosophy specifically, see `PressfieldFramework.md`. For character psychology, see `StorrFramework.md`.

---

## The Three Acts

| Act | Range | Purpose | Pressfield's Clothesline |
|-----|-------|---------|--------------------------|
| **Act I: Setup** | 0-25% | Establish world, character, flaw; introduce concept and conflict | String the clothesline — hook + concept + stakes |
| **Act II-A: Ascending Action** | 25-50% | Hero engages with the new world; escalating complications | Hang the major items — complications and escalation |
| **Act II-B: Descending Action** | 50-75% | Forces close in; hero's flaw exposed; mounting pressure | The clothesline bows under weight — crisis builds |
| **Act III: Resolution** | 75-100% | Transformation tested; climax; new equilibrium | Pull taut and snap — climax resolves the concept |

## Act I: Setup (0-25%)

### Phase 1: Opening Image / Ordinary World (0-5%)

**What happens:** The "before" snapshot. The hero in their normal environment, operating within their sacred flaw. The world appears stable.

**Mandatory events:**
- **Opening Image** (0-1%): A single visual or scene that encapsulates the hero's current state. This will be mirrored by the Final Image to show transformation.
- **Character Introduction**: The protagonist's behavior demonstrates (not states) their sacred flaw.

**Layer priorities:** World, Character Change, Prose (establish voice/tone)

### Phase 2: Theme and Setup (5-10%)

**What happens:** The world is fully established. Key relationships are introduced. Someone (not the protagonist) hints at the theme.

**Mandatory events:**
- **Theme Stated** (~5%): A secondary character articulates the theme-question, often casually. The protagonist doesn't recognize its importance. (Pressfield: the theme-question planted early.)
- **Supporting Cast Introduction**: Key allies, mentors, and the antagonist's presence (directly or by shadow).

**Layer priorities:** Meaning, Relationships, World

### Phase 3: Catalyst / Inciting Incident (10-12%)

**What happens:** An event disrupts the ordinary world. This is not the hero's choice — it happens TO them. It directly targets the sacred flaw.

**Mandatory events:**
- **Inciting Incident / Catalyst** (~10%): The event that upsets the balance. Must be specific, concrete, and impossible to ignore. (Story Grid: causal or coincidental inciting incident.)

**Layer priorities:** Plot, Character Change, Mystery (first major question opens)

### Phase 4: Debate / Refusal (12-20%)

**What happens:** The hero resists the call. This is the sacred flaw's defense mechanism in action — the hero tries to maintain their flawed model of the world.

**Mandatory events:**
- **Debate Sequence** (12-20%): The hero weighs options, seeks alternatives, tries to avoid commitment. This is NOT padding — it demonstrates the strength of the flaw's grip.

**Layer priorities:** Character Change, Relationships

### Phase 5: Break Into Two / First Plot Point (20-25%)

**What happens:** The hero commits to the new world/approach. This is a decision, not an event — the hero CHOOSES to cross the threshold. The old world is left behind.

**Mandatory events:**
- **First Plot Point / Break Into Two** (~25%): An active choice by the protagonist to engage with the conflict. The act boundary. (Vogler: Crossing the Threshold. Field: Plot Point I.)

**Layer priorities:** Plot, Character Change

## Act II-A: Ascending Action (25-50%)

### Phase 6: B-Story and New World (25-30%)

**What happens:** New characters enter. The B-Story (usually a relationship that will teach the theme) begins. The hero explores the unfamiliar world.

**Mandatory events:**
- **B-Story Introduction** (~25-30%): A new relationship begins — often the love interest, mentor, or unexpected ally. This relationship will eventually deliver the thematic lesson.

**Layer priorities:** Relationships, Meaning, World

### Phase 7: Fun and Games / Tests and Allies (30-45%)

**What happens:** The promise of the premise is delivered. The hero applies their skills to the new world. Early victories (or entertaining failures). The audience gets what they came for.

**Mandatory events:**
- **Promise of the Premise**: Multiple scenes delivering on the concept's promise. If the concept is "dinosaur theme park," this is where we see dinosaurs.
- **First Pinch Point** (~37%): A reminder of the antagonist's power and threat. The villain (or opposition force) flexes, demonstrating what's at stake. (Pressfield: the villain IS the story.)

**Layer priorities:** Plot, World, Mystery (secondary mysteries develop)

### Phase 8: Midpoint / Point of No Return (50%)

**What happens:** The structural pivot. A major revelation or reversal that transforms the nature of the conflict. The hero can no longer go back.

**Mandatory events:**
- **Midpoint** (50%): Either a false victory (things seem great but aren't) or a false defeat (things seem terrible but contain a seed of hope). The stakes escalate permanently. (Harmon: "Find what they wanted." McKee: "Midpoint reversal.")

**What changes at the midpoint:**
- The hero shifts from reactive to proactive (or vice versa)
- New information reframes everything
- The personal and public stakes merge
- The ticking clock activates or accelerates

**Layer priorities:** Plot, Mystery (major reveal or redirect), Character Change

## Act II-B: Descending Action (50-75%)

### Phase 9: Bad Guys Close In (50-62%)

**What happens:** Internal and external forces tighten. The team fractures. Allies waver. The villain gains ground. The hero's flaw creates escalating problems.

**Mandatory events:**
- **Escalating Complications** (50-62%): A series of progressive complications where each attempt to solve the problem makes things worse. The hero's flawed approach is increasingly exposed.
- **Second Pinch Point** (~62%): Another reminder of the antagonist's power, more visceral than the first. The villain's full capability is revealed.

**Layer priorities:** Character Change (flaw under pressure), Relationships (bonds tested), Plot

### Phase 10: All Is Lost (75%)

**What happens:** The nadir. Everything the hero has tried using their flawed worldview has failed. A "whiff of death" — something or someone connected to the hero dies or is destroyed (literally or metaphorically).

**Mandatory events:**
- **All Is Lost** (~75%): The lowest point. The old model shatters. (Pressfield: every flaw-based strategy has failed. Storr: the flawed model must completely break.)

**Layer priorities:** Character Change (sacred flaw fully exposed), Meaning

### Phase 11: Dark Night of the Soul (75-80%)

**What happens:** The emotional aftermath of All Is Lost. Despair, reflection, and finally the decision to try something fundamentally different. This is where the B-Story lesson arrives.

**Mandatory events:**
- **Dark Night of the Soul** (75-80%): The hero sits with their failure. In this silence, the B-Story relationship delivers the thematic insight — often without realizing it. The hero sees the truth.
- **Second Plot Point / Break Into Three** (~80%): The hero synthesizes want and need. A new approach emerges — one that requires abandoning the sacred flaw. (Field: Plot Point II.)

**Layer priorities:** Character Change (moment of truth), Meaning (theme crystallizes), Relationships (B-Story delivers)

---

## Act III: Resolution (80-100%)

### Phase 12: Finale (80-95%)

**What happens:** The transformed (or transforming) hero enacts a new plan. The climax tests whether the transformation is genuine.

**Mandatory events:**
- **The Five-Point Finale** (Snyder's structure):
  1. **Gathering the Team** — The hero assembles resources using new understanding
  2. **Executing the Plan** — The new approach is put into action
  3. **High Tower Surprise** — An unexpected obstacle threatens everything (the villain's last move)
  4. **Dig Deep Down** — The hero must reach into their transformed self for the final push
  5. **Execution of New Plan** — The hero defeats the opposition by embodying the theme's answer

- **Climax** (~90%): The confrontation between hero and villain that answers the theme-question through action. (Pressfield: hero defeats villain by proving the theme's thesis. Storr: the crisis forces the final choice between flaw and need.)

**Layer priorities:** All seven layers converge — this is the story's culmination

### Phase 13: Final Image / Resolution (95-100%)

**What happens:** The "after" snapshot. The new equilibrium. The hero in possession of the Gift.

**Mandatory events:**
- **Resolution / Denouement** (95-99%): Loose ends resolved. New status quo established. The world has changed because the hero changed (positive arc) or the hero is destroyed because they refused to change (tragedy).
- **Final Image** (99-100%): Mirrors the Opening Image to demonstrate transformation. (Pressfield: the Gift is visible — the hero lives differently.)

**Layer priorities:** Meaning (final thematic statement through image), Character Change (transformation visible), World (new equilibrium)

## Using This Map

### During Story Bible Construction (BuildBible)

1. Map your story's major events to this timeline
2. For each phase, identify what happens across all seven layers (see `StoryLayers.md`)
3. Verify every mandatory event is planned
4. Check that the Pressfield elements are in place (concept → clothesline → hook → villain → all is lost → gift)

### During Chapter Writing (WriteChapter)

1. Identify which phase(s) the chapter covers
2. Load the mandatory events for that phase
3. Check the layer priorities for that phase
4. Write with awareness of where you are on the tension curve

### Phase Checklist for Story Bible

- [ ] Opening Image planned (mirrors Final Image)
- [ ] Theme-Question stated by secondary character
- [ ] Inciting Incident targets the sacred flaw
- [ ] Debate demonstrates flaw's grip
- [ ] First Plot Point is hero's active CHOICE
